# Writing

Write like a technical peer talking to another technical peer. Confident, specific, concrete. The reader knows how to read, so you do not need to oversell.

## The standard

All prose on the docs site, in the blog, and in published external material is held to the standard described in Wikipedia's [Signs of AI writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing). If a sentence reads like it was written to impress a search engine or a board deck, rewrite it.

## Hard rules

- **No em dashes.** Use commas, periods, or parentheses. This rule has no exceptions.
- **Straight quotes only.** No curly quotes, no smart quotes. Configure your editor.
- **Sentence case in headings.** "Writing for the docs", not "Writing For The Docs". The web stopped using title case years ago.
- **Code references take a path and a line number.** `src/components/SlideDeck/index.tsx:42` is precise. "the slide component" is not.

## Voice and tone

Write like you talk in a code review, not like you talk in a sales pitch. The reader is a peer who can tell when you are filling space.

- Confident, not boastful. State what is true. Skip "we are excited to announce".
- Specific, not vague. Name the thing, the version, the file. "Recent improvements" tells the reader nothing.
- Concrete, not abstract. "The build runs in 14 seconds" beats "the build is fast".
- Plain, not elevated. "Use" beats "leverage". "Build" beats "architect a solution for".

## Keep a pulse

Clean prose is half the job. Voiceless prose is the other mistake. A page can be free of em dashes and puffery and still read like it came out of a template.

- **Have an opinion.** If you are describing a tradeoff, name which side you lean. "Both are fine" is usually the wrong answer.
- **Vary rhythm.** Short sentences. Then longer ones that wind through a thought. Not every paragraph needs the same shape.
- **Use "I" when you mean it.** First person is not unprofessional. It is honest.
- **Let one or two tangents live.** If a half-formed aside is interesting, keep it. Too-tidy prose reads as synthetic.

## Patterns to avoid

These are the tells that make prose read as machine-written or marketing-written. Watch for them in your own drafts.

- **Inflated symbolism.** "A testament to", "stands as a", "embodies". The thing is the thing. It does not stand as anything.
- **Promotional language.** "Crucial", "pivotal", "vital", "key", "groundbreaking", "transformative". If everything is crucial, nothing is.
- **Puffery vocabulary.** "Landscape", "tapestry", "ecosystem", "vibrant", "robust", "seamless". Reach for a real noun instead.
- **Vague attributions.** "Many experts believe", "it is widely accepted". Cite the source or drop the claim.
- **Negative parallelisms.** "Not just X, but Y". "More than just". The framing is filler.
- **Excessive conjunctive phrases.** "Moreover", "furthermore", "in addition". One per page is plenty. Usually zero.
- **Sycophancy.** "Great question". "Excellent point". The reader did not ask for praise.
- **Generic conclusions.** "Exciting times ahead". "The journey continues". End on substance or do not end at all.
- **Rule of three.** Three adjectives, three verbs, three clauses. Vary the rhythm.
- **Superficial -ing analyses.** "Demonstrating", "showcasing", "highlighting". Pick a stronger verb or drop the gerund.
- **Copula avoidance.** "Serves as", "stands as", "functions as", "acts as". Use "is". A hammer is a tool. It does not serve as one.
- **Elegant variation.** The protagonist, then the main character, then the central figure, then the hero. Pick one name for the entity and keep using it. The thesaurus is a trap.
- **False ranges.** "From X to Y, from A to B" when X, Y, A, and B are not on a meaningful scale. The framing wants to sound comprehensive. It reads as filler.
- **Inline-header lists.** Bullets where each item starts with a bolded phrase followed by a colon and one sentence. The bolded phrases usually recreate a heading the paragraph already has. Write the paragraph. (This list is an exception: each item names a distinct pattern. When the bolded chunks are not a taxonomy, cut the bolding.)
- **Knowledge-cutoff disclaimers.** "As of my last training", "based on available information", "while specific details are limited". These are chatbot artifacts. If the information is stale, say when it was verified and move on.
- **Filler phrases.** "In order to" is "to". "Due to the fact that" is "because". "At this point in time" is "now". "Has the ability to" is "can". Cut the scaffolding.
- **Excessive hedging.** "It could potentially possibly be argued that". Pick a confidence level and state it once. "Probably" or "may" is usually enough.
- **Media name-dropping.** "Featured in The New York Times, Wired, and The Verge." Name-dropping without context is noise. If a source said something specific, cite what they said. If not, drop it.
- **Challenges-and-future-prospects sections.** "Despite challenges, the project continues to thrive." Formulaic filler. State the specific challenges and what is being done about them, or do not include a section at all.

## Prose over bullets

Bullets are for enumerable lists: steps, options, reference rows. They are not a substitute for thinking through how an idea connects to the next one.

When you find yourself writing four bullets that each start with the same word, that is a paragraph. Write the paragraph.

## Length and density

Short is harder than long. Aim for short.

- One concept per page. Link to related ones instead of growing a monolith.
- One idea per paragraph. If a paragraph has two ideas, split it.
- Cut every sentence that does not change what the reader knows.
