# Images

Images carry weight. A bad one tells the reader the page was rushed. A good one earns the space it takes.

## Editorial over stock

Reach for editorial photography first. A real workshop, a real desk, a real piece of paper with real notes on it. Skip the businessperson-shaking-hands shelf entirely. The reader has seen it.

If a photograph is going to ship in a hero slot or a blog post, it should feel like it belongs to the project, like someone went and took it on purpose.

## Subjects that fit

The work this project is about is technical and material. Lean into that.

- Workspaces that look used. Tools out, papers stacked, screens at angles.
- Real materials. Wood, metal, paper, fabric, clay. Texture beats gloss.
- People doing something. Hands on a keyboard, a pen mid-stroke, a head down at a workbench. Not a posed grin at the camera.
- Places where work happens. Studios, kitchens, garages, shop floors, archive rooms.

## Light and color

Natural light, soft shadows, no green spill from a fluorescent ceiling. Time of day shows. Window light reads as honest. Studio softboxes read as marketing.

Color sits on the warm side more often than the cool side, but neither is wrong. Muted before saturated. If you need a brand accent in the frame, one accent is plenty.

## When to use what

- **Photograph.** Hero slots, blog post headers, anything that benefits from texture and atmosphere. Use `<IdealImage>` for these (see the [MDX reference](./mdx-reference)). The responsive sizing and blur placeholder are worth it.
- **Illustration.** Concepts that have no obvious physical analogue. Decision trees, mental models, mood pieces. Lead with the medium so the model does not drift toward photorealism.
- **Diagram.** Relationships, flows, sequences. See [diagrams](./diagrams) for the aesthetic direction there.

If a photograph and a diagram could both work, the diagram is usually the better choice. It carries information; the photograph carries mood.

## What to avoid

- Clip art and cartoon characters. They date instantly and sit oddly next to technical content.
- Generic "tech" stock: glowing networks, hands typing on a backlit keyboard, abstract data swirls. The reader has seen all of these and learned to skip them.
- Gradient blob illustrations. Every SaaS landing page from 2018 to 2023 used the same five.
- Heavy filters. If the photograph needs a filter to look interesting, it is the wrong photograph.
