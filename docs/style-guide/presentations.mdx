# Presentations

A deck is for ordered talking points with a clear flow. The speaker carries the load. The slide anchors a single idea so the audience can hold it while the speaker fills in the rest.

If there is no speaker, there is probably no deck.

## When a deck is the right format

- The material has a sequence the audience needs to follow in order.
- One idea benefits from sitting alone for a beat before the next one arrives.
- The pacing matters. Pauses, builds, returns.
- The audience will see this once, live, and not return to it.

## When a doc is the right format

- The material is reference. People will scan, search, and return.
- The structure is dense. The reader needs to see two ideas next to each other, not in sequence.
- The content needs to be searchable. Decks trap text inside JavaScript and surface poorly to search.
- The content will live longer than a quarter. Decks rot fast. The link rots faster.

When in doubt, write the doc. A deck can pull from a doc later. A doc cannot pull from a deck.

## Density

One idea per slide. If the slide has three ideas, that is three slides. The temptation to consolidate is the temptation to lose the audience.

A slide that needs two paragraphs of body copy is a doc that wandered into the wrong format.

## Pacing

The speaker fills in. The slide anchors. If the slide already says everything, the speaker is reading aloud, and the audience is reading ahead. Both lose.

A working slide has a title, a single anchor (an image, a short list, a sentence), and silence around it.
